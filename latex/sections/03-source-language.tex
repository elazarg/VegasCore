\section{The source language and its visibility discipline}
\label{sec:source-language}

The carrier of game-theoretic meaning is the event graph, but the
event graph is not what a programmer writes.  This section describes
what a programmer writes: a small typed protocol calculus with
five constructors, a visibility annotation on every binding, and
five static obligations that turn a syntactic tree into a checked
game description.  Two design decisions are made here that propagate
through the rest of the construction: visibility is annotated on
bindings rather than on uses, and well-formedness is split into
five independent predicates rather than one monolithic condition.

\subsection{Source syntax}
\label{sec:source:syntax}

\subsubsection*{The expression layer}

Programs are written over a fixed but abstract \emph{expression
language} $L$, which packages a type universe $\Ty$, a value family
$\Val : \Ty \to \mathbf{Type}$ with decidable equality, an
expression syntax $\Expr(\Gamma, \tau)$, an evaluator
$\mathrm{eval}: \Expr(\Gamma,\tau) \times \Env(\Gamma) \to
\Val(\tau)$, and an analogous distribution syntax $\mathrm{DistExpr}
(\Gamma, \tau)$ with evaluator into $\FWeight{\Val(\tau)}$.  Each
expression carries a syntactic dependency set $\mathrm{deps}(e)
\subseteq \mathrm{vars}(\Gamma)$, and the language is required to
prove the soundness law
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eval-soundness}
  \rho_1 \mathrel{\equiv_{\mathrm{deps}(e)}} \rho_2
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  \mathrm{eval}(e, \rho_1) = \mathrm{eval}(e, \rho_2).
\end{equation}

The codebase ships a concrete instance with $\Ty = \{\intt, \bool,
\mathsf{range}\,\ell\,h, \mathsf{option}\,b\}$ and the obvious
constants, $+$, $=$, conjunction, negation, conditional, and option
operators; this is sufficient for the worked examples.  Other
expression languages may be substituted as long as
(\ref{eq:eval-soundness}) holds.

\designnote{The expression language is an abstraction boundary
because the protocol-level theorems hold uniformly in the choice of
syntactic primitives.  Vegas does not inspect expressions; it relies
on $\mathrm{deps}$ being a sound over-approximation of semantic
reads.  An instance may over-report dependencies, but it cannot
under-report a semantically relevant read.}

\subsubsection*{Visibility-tagged bindings}

A \emph{visibility tag} on a binding is one of
\[
  \mathit{vis} \;::=\; \pub \;\mid\; \hid\,p,
\]
where $p \in \Players$ is the unique \emph{owner}.  A typed,
visibility-aware context is a list of triples
$(x, \mathit{vis}, \tau)$.  Two derived contexts are used heavily:
\begin{align*}
  \pubctx{\Gamma} &= \bigl[(x, \pub, \tau) \in \Gamma\bigr]
    && \text{the public sublist}, \\
  \view{p}        &= \bigl[(x, \mathit{vis}, \tau) \in \Gamma
    \mid \mathit{vis} = \pub \text{ or } \mathit{vis} = \hid\,p\bigr]
    && \text{the view of player $p$}.
\end{align*}
Two erasures forget the visibility tag: $\erasectx{\Gamma}$ keeps
all bindings, $\erasepub{\Gamma}$ keeps only public ones.  The two
projections commute,
\(
  \erasectx{\pubctx{\Gamma}} = \erasepub{\Gamma},
\)
which is what allows results about $\erasepub{\Gamma}$-shaped goals
to be discharged by reasoning about $\erasectx{\Gamma}$-shaped
ones.

\subsubsection*{The five constructors}

A core program is a typed sequence of \emph{events} terminated by
$\Ret$:
\[
\arraycolsep=1pt
\begin{array}{r@{\;}c@{\;}l}
\Ret\,\overline{p}
  & : & \mathsf{VegasCore}\,\Gamma, \\
\Let\,x = e;\,k
  & : & \Expr(\erasepub{\Gamma}, b) \times
       \mathsf{VegasCore}((x,\pub\,b)::\Gamma)
       \to \mathsf{VegasCore}\,\Gamma, \\
\Sample\,x \sim D;\,k
  & : & \mathrm{DistExpr}(\erasepub{\Gamma}, b) \times
       \mathsf{VegasCore}((x,\pub\,b)::\Gamma)
       \to \mathsf{VegasCore}\,\Gamma, \\
\Commit_{p}\,x \mid R;\,k
  & : & \Expr((x,b)::\erasectx{\Gamma}, \bool) \times
       \mathsf{VegasCore}((x,\hid\,p\,b)::\Gamma)
       \to \mathsf{VegasCore}\,\Gamma, \\
\Reveal\,y := x;\,k
  & : & \mathrm{HasVar}\,\Gamma\,x\,(\hid\,p\,b) \times
       \mathsf{VegasCore}((y,\pub\,b)::\Gamma)
       \to \mathsf{VegasCore}\,\Gamma.
\end{array}
\]
The four non-administrative constructors model the observable
activity of a multi-party computation.  $\Sample$ is a public coin.
$\Commit$ is a player's sealed choice, with a guard $R$ that
restricts which values are legal.  $\Reveal$ makes a sealed value
public.  $\Ret$ terminates and pays out.  $\Let$ is the only
administrative form; we keep it for ergonomic and structural reasons
discussed below.

The argument types enforce the visibility discipline syntactically.
$\Let$ and $\Sample$ types their bodies over $\erasepub{\Gamma}$, so
they cannot mention hidden state.  $\Reveal$ takes a typed
membership $\mathrm{HasVar}\,\Gamma\,x\,(\hid\,p\,b)$, ensuring the
revealed name is in scope and is hidden to player $p$.  $\Commit$
allows its guard to mention the entire erased context — this is by
design; see the next subsection.

\designnote{Two visibility tags ($\pub, \hid\,p$) make the model
\emph{owner-or-all}: there is no group-visibility constructor.  This
is sufficient for the imperfect-information patterns standard in
mechanism design (sealed bids, private signals, hidden card hands)
and avoids a combinatorial explosion of view operators.  Reveal
operations promote a hidden value to a public alias visible to
all players, so they cannot be sequenced to expose a value to a
proper subgroup; genuine group visibility is deliberately out of
scope (\S\ref{sec:disc:ext}).}

\designnote{$\Let\,x=e$ is semantically equivalent to
$\Sample\,x\sim\delta_e$, but we keep it as a distinct constructor
so that the expression language need not expose a lift
$\Expr \to \mathrm{DistExpr}$, so that program size is linear in the
surface text, and so that strategy types depend only on the
binding's visibility, not on whether its value happens to be
deterministic.}

\subsection{The visibility discipline for guards}
\label{sec:source:guards}

The $\Commit$ guard $R$ types over the full erased context
$(x,b)::\erasectx{\Gamma}$, allowing it to syntactically mention
\emph{any} variable in scope, including hidden state owned by other
players.  This is plainly stronger than the strategic intent: the
acting player should not be able to condition a commit on opponents'
hidden values.  We resolve the gap by carrying a separate predicate
\emph{about the guard's dependency set} rather than restricting the
typing rule:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:guarduses}
  \mathrm{GuardUsesOnly}_p(R)
  \;\stackrel{\mathrm{def}}{=}\;
  \mathrm{deps}(R) \subseteq \{x\} \cup \mathrm{visibleVars}(p, \Gamma).
\end{equation}
The pivotal soundness lemma is then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:guard-obs-eq}
  \rho_1 \mathrel{\equiv_{\mathrm{visibleVars}(p,\Gamma)}} \rho_2
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  \mathrm{eval}(R, [x\mapsto a], \rho_1)
  = \mathrm{eval}(R, [x\mapsto a], \rho_2)
\end{equation}
for any candidate action $a$ — provided the introduced action
variable $x$ is fresh in $\Gamma$ (so it does not shadow an existing
binding).

\designnote{The split between typing and dependency-restriction is
intentional.  A typing rule that forbade the guard from mentioning
hidden variables would force the user to write a dedicated guard
expression over a freshly projected context — adding a syntactic
boundary that the elaborator would have to undo.  Carrying
$\mathrm{GuardUsesOnly}_p$ as a side predicate keeps the surface
syntax uniform while preserving the strategic invariant
(\ref{eq:guard-obs-eq}).}

\subsection{Surface ergonomics}
\label{sec:source:surface}

The surface language $\mathsf{VegasLang}$ is a strict elaboration
over the core: it mirrors the five core constructors, plus two
ergonomic additions.

\paragraph{Simultaneous yield phases.}
A simultaneous phase records a list of same-phase
commit-then-reveal pairs by distinct actors.  Lowering emits all
phase commitments first (in source order) and only afterwards emits
the corresponding reveals; no commit in the same phase reads any
other commit's name.  After lowering, the elaborator computes the
phase's commit/reveal information barrier explicitly: the same-phase
commits share an event-graph frontier, but each reveal waits for
\emph{all} earlier commits, not only for its own committed payload.

\paragraph{Nullable yields.}
A surface yield is a public-move sugar where the revealed value has
type $\mathsf{option}\,b$.  Lowering changes the internal commit
payload type from $b$ to $\mathsf{option}\,b$ and lifts the
user-supplied guard $R$ on $b$ to an internal guard
$\mathsf{nullableCommitGuard}(R)$ on $\mathsf{option}\,b$ that
agrees with $R$ on $\mathsf{some}\,v$ and accepts $\mathsf{none}$
unconditionally.  Feasibility — \emph{Legal} below — is then
discharged by choosing $\mathsf{none}$.

\designnote{Nullable yields are the protocol-level mechanism for
making an infeasible guard \emph{satisfiable by aborting}.  The
strategic cost of aborting is intentionally not specified by the
constructor: the revealed public has option type, so payoff
expressions are forced by the type system to decide how the
$\mathsf{none}$ branch affects later utilities.  A protocol designer
can punish abort, reward abort, or leave it neutral; the language
makes the decision explicit instead of letting it default silently.}

\subsection{Static well-formedness}
\label{sec:source:wf}

A raw $\mathsf{VegasCore}$ term is a tree.  Five static obligations
turn it into a game description:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Contextual well-formedness} ($\mathrm{WFCtx}$): the
  initial context has no repeated names.
\item \emph{Fresh bindings}: every binder introduced by $\Let$,
  $\Sample$, $\Commit$, or $\Reveal$ is fresh in its enclosing
  context.  This is SSA: every name is bound at exactly one program
  point.
\item \emph{Reveal completeness}: every committed value is revealed
  exactly once before the program reaches $\Ret$.
\item \emph{Distribution normalization}: every $\Sample$
  distribution sums to weight $1$.
\item \emph{Legality}: at every $\Commit$ site for player $p$ with
  guard $R$ over type $b$ and ambient erased environment $\rho$,
  there exists a value $a \in \Val(b)$ with
  $\mathrm{eval}(R, [x\mapsto a], \rho) = \mathsf{true}$.
\end{enumerate}
The bundle of a context, an initial environment, a checked program,
and these five obligations is called a \emph{checked program}.  All
public game-theoretic APIs consume a checked program; raw
$\mathsf{VegasCore}$ is never a public input.

\designnote{The five obligations are independent.  Freshness and
$\mathrm{WFCtx}$ are static and decidable.  Reveal completeness is a
finite tree-walk, also decidable.  Distribution normalization reduces
to checking finitely-many rational sums.  Legality is decidable when
value domains are finite, which they are for all checked examples
and is the standing assumption of the strategic API.  We resist the
temptation to fold them into a single \emph{good-program} predicate:
keeping them separate makes failures locally diagnosable and lets
intermediate constructions (event graph, machine) require only the
subsets they actually need.}
