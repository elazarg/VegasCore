\section{Frontier strategies and kernel games}
\label{sec:frontier-strategies}

\subsection{Native round views}

Building on the checkpoint primitives of \Cref{sec:mach:checkpoints},
the round view is the strategic-level presentation of the primitive
graph machine.  A round view supplies an active-player set, per-player
available round actions, a legal joint-action predicate, a transition
kernel, and a primitive event-batch witness for refinement reasoning.
We call these games \emph{native} to distinguish the original round-view
denotation from the FOSG and EFG presentations derived in
\Cref{sec:presentations}.

The \emph{frontier} is the boundary between completed graph work and
choices still waiting to be made.  For a compiled program, the canonical
frontier round is:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*),leftmargin=1.7em,itemsep=1pt]
\item close any ready internal graph nodes;
\item if commit nodes are ready, ask active players for actions, execute
  the selected commit frontier, and close newly ready internal nodes.
\end{enumerate}
Graph well-formedness and guard liveness rule out the case of a
nonterminal state with no internal work and no active commits.

This is a checkpoint semantics, not a primitive scheduler.  Primitive
order inside the event batch is a proof witness used to connect to
machines and refinements.  The player history records checkpoint
observations.

\subsection{Strategies}
\label{sec:frontier-strategies-strategies}

The bounded round view yields finite reachable information states for
each player.  A pure frontier strategy maps each reachable information
state to an optional round action.  A behavioral frontier strategy maps
each reachable information state to a PMF over optional round actions.
The legal carriers contain only strategies that choose available actions
at every reachable information state.  Illegal actions are not filtered
after the fact; they are absent from the strategy type used by the
kernel game.

Thus a pure strategy is a complete contingency plan: it says what the
player prescribes at every reachable view, even views that may not occur
under the final profile.  A behavioral strategy is a local randomized
rule at each reachable view.  The product mixed-pure game is the
independent mixed extension of the pure frontier game: each player
samples a pure contingent plan once, independently of the other players,
and then the sampled pure profile is run through the pure frontier
outcome kernel.  This is the carrier used for finite Kuhn statements.

\subsection{Kernel games}
\label{sec:fosg-kernel-games}

A kernel game contains strategy carriers, an outcome type, a
profile-to-outcome PMF, and a utility function:
\[
  \KG = (\{S_p\}_{p\in\Players}, \Outcome, K, u).
\]
VegasCore exposes the following canonical checked-program games:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Name & Strategies & Outcomes \\
\midrule
\(\Kpure(g)\) & reachable pure frontier & terminal payoff vectors \\
\(\Kbeh(g)\) & reachable behavioral frontier & terminal payoff vectors \\
\(\Kmix(g)\) & product mixed-pure & terminal payoff vectors \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

There are also \emph{completed}-frontier and \emph{history} variants used to
prove source adequacy, trace adequacy, and the bridge theorems.  A
completed-frontier game is the frontier process run to graph completion ---
every node finished, so every outcome is terminal --- presented as a single
game; the history variants additionally retain the run history.  These are
proof surfaces rather than separate user-facing denotations.

To present completion as a \emph{total} game the construction runs to a fixed
horizon equal to the instruction count and supplies a default outcome at that
horizon.  This does not reintroduce the silent-zero payoff that
\Cref{sec:machines} rules out: the run provably terminates before the horizon
is reached, so the default outcome lies outside the support of every reachable
play.  The default is a totalization device, exactly like the machine's stutter
branch, never an assigned payoff.

The distinction matters for reading the code.  The public user-facing
objects are the payoff-vector frontier games.  The completed and history
games retain more execution structure so the proofs can show exactly how
payoffs, FOSG histories, EFG histories, and runtime traces line up.

\subsection{Solution concepts}
\label{sec:fosg-solution}

Solution concepts are inherited from the generic game-theory library and
specialized to the frontier games.  The public theorem modules expose
Nash, mixed Nash, epsilon-Nash, best response, weak and strict dominance,
dominance-solvability, correlated and coarse correlated equilibrium,
Pareto efficiency, individual rationality, rationalizability, exact and
ordinal potentials~\citep{MondererShapley1996}, social welfare, price of
anarchy/stability, guarantees, security levels, saddle points,
constant-sum, zero-sum, minimax-theorem checks~\citep{vonNeumannMinimax},
and team-game predicates.

The specialization states the concepts at the level a Vegas programmer
sees: the checked source program and its canonical frontier games.
For example, a statement that a checked program has a
\TT{BehavioralFrontierNash} profile is a statement about the
strategy surface induced by the compiled event graph, not about a
manually written normal-form payoff table.

\leancorr{Round-view semantics are in \TT{Vegas.Frontier.RoundView} and
\TT{Vegas.Frontier.Games}; the canonical checked-program games
are \TT{pureFrontierGame}, \TT{behavioralFrontierGame}, and
\TT{mixedPureFrontierGame}.  Program-facing solution-concept wrappers
are in \TT{Vegas.Frontier.SolutionConcepts} and
\TT{Vegas.Theorems.SolutionConcepts}.  The completed-game horizon and its
source-side termination-at-horizon lemmas (under which the default outcome is
unreachable) are in the source-frontier development under
