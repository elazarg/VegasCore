\section{Frontier strategies and game forms}
\label{sec:fosg-strategies}

\subsection{Native round views}

The Vegas-facing game semantics are native frontier round views over the
primitive graph machine.  A round view supplies an active-player set,
per-player available round actions, a legal joint-action predicate, a
transition kernel, and a primitive event-batch witness for refinement
reasoning.

For a compiled program, the canonical frontier round has two phases.
If internal graph nodes are ready, the round closes internal work before
presenting a strategic choice.  Otherwise, if commit nodes are ready,
each active player chooses actions for their ready commit nodes; the
round executes the selected commit frontier and then closes newly ready
internal work.  If neither internal work nor active commits exist and
the state is nonterminal, graph well-formedness plus guard liveness rule
out the case.

This is a checkpoint semantics, not a primitive scheduler.  Primitive
order inside the event batch is a proof witness used to connect to
machines and refinements.  The player history records checkpoint
observations.

\subsection{Strategies}
\label{sec:fosg:strategies}

The bounded round view yields finite reachable information states for
each player.  A pure frontier strategy maps each reachable information
state to an optional round action.  A behavioral frontier strategy maps
each reachable information state to a PMF over optional round actions.
The legal carriers contain only strategies that choose available actions
at every reachable information state.  Illegal actions are not filtered
after the fact; they are absent from the strategy type used by the
kernel game.

The product mixed-pure game is the independent mixed extension of the
pure frontier game.  It is the carrier used for finite Kuhn statements:
each player samples a pure contingent plan once, independently of the
other players, and then the sampled pure profile is run through the pure
frontier outcome kernel.

\subsection{Kernel games}
\label{sec:fosg:kernel-games}

A kernel game contains strategy carriers, an outcome type, a
profile-to-outcome PMF, and a utility function:
\[
  \KG = (\{S_p\}_{p\in\Players}, O, K, u).
\]
VegasCore exposes the following canonical checked-program games:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lp{0.56\textwidth}@{}}
\toprule
Name & Meaning \\
\midrule
\(\mathrm{pureFrontierGame}(g)\) &
  pure reachable frontier strategies, terminal payoff-vector outcomes. \\
\(\mathrm{behavioralFrontierGame}(g)\) &
  behavioral reachable frontier strategies, terminal payoff-vector outcomes. \\
\(\mathrm{mixedPureFrontierGame}(g)\) &
  product mixed pure strategies, terminal payoff-vector outcomes. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

There are also completed/history variants used to prove source adequacy,
trace adequacy, and bridge theorems.  These are proof surfaces rather than
separate user-facing denotations.

\subsection{Solution concepts}
\label{sec:fosg:solution}

Solution concepts are inherited from the generic game-theory library and
specialized to the frontier games.  The public theorem modules expose
Nash, epsilon-Nash, best response, weak and strict dominance,
dominance-solvability, correlated and coarse correlated equilibrium,
Pareto efficiency, individual rationality, rationalizability, exact and
ordinal potentials, social welfare, price of anarchy/stability,
guarantees, security levels, saddle points, constant-sum, zero-sum, and
team-game predicates.

The specialization states the concepts at the level a Vegas programmer
sees: the checked source program and its canonical frontier games.
