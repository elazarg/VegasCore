\section{Presentations: extensive form and Kuhn simulations}
\label{sec:presentations}

The kernel games of \S\ref{sec:fosg-strategies} are the primary
strategic-form interface.  Two further results are needed to make
the construction interoperate with the textbook tradition: an
extensive-form presentation of the FOSG view, and finite Kuhn
realization/deviation theorems that link mixed and behavioral strategies.
The native strategic layer is not defined through these presentations;
their connection to native strategies is proved as native/FOSG transport.

\subsection{The canonical EFG presentation}
\label{sec:pres:efg}

For each checked program $g$, the codebase produces a canonical
extensive-form game $\mathsf{efg}(g)$ by serializing the bounded
event-graph FOSG view: each FOSG round becomes a block of $|
\Players|$ sequential decision opportunities (each over the player's
local round-action alphabet, augmented with $\mathsf{none}$ for
inactive players) followed by a chance node implementing the FOSG
transition.

\begin{theorem}[EFG presentation adequacy]
\label{thm:efg}
For every checked program $g$ and every bounded event-graph FOSG
behavioral profile $\beta$, the translated EFG outcome kernel
projected to the payoff-vector outcome agrees with the bounded FOSG
outcome kernel:
\[
  \mathrm{map}(\mathrm{publicOf})
    \,\bigl(\mathrm{outcomeKernel}_{\mathsf{efg}(g)}\,\beta\bigr)
  \;=\;
  K^{\mathrm{FOSG}}_{\mathrm{beh}}(g)(\beta),
\]
where $\beta$ is first translated by the canonical FOSG-to-EFG profile
translation.
\end{theorem}

The proof is by induction on the bounded FOSG depth, exploiting the
fact that translated legal FOSG behavioral strategies put all mass
on $\mathsf{none}$ for inactive players, so the administrative
decision opportunities at the EFG layer add no strategic freedom.

\designnote{The EFG bridge is intentionally a \emph{view}, not the
semantic owner.  The authoritative semantics is the event-graph
machine; the EFG is a presentation that respects the payoff-vector
outcome law of the bounded FOSG presentation.  Relating that FOSG law
to the native Vegas strategy carrier is handled by the native/FOSG
outcome-law transport theorem.  This preserves the two
strategically irrelevant features of the EFG — the arbitrary
linearization of independent same-round events, and the introduction of
administrative no-op decision opportunities — without making them part
of the underlying game.}

\subsection{Mixed extension and finite Kuhn simulations}
\label{sec:pres:kuhn}

For a finite player set, the \emph{mixed extension} of a kernel game
$\KG$ replaces each strategy space $S_p$ by $\PMF{S_p}$ and pushes the
outcome kernel through the independent product:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mixed-extension}
  \Kmix(\mu)
  \;=\;
  \bigl({\textstyle\bigotimes_p}\,\mu_p\bigr) \bind \Kpure.
\end{equation}

Kuhn's classical theorem~\citep{Kuhn1953} says that in finite
extensive-form games with perfect recall, mixed strategies over pure
contingent plans and behavioral strategies are realizationally
equivalent.  The bounded event-graph FOSG presentation carries two
forms of this theorem: a realization equality and a Nash
deviation-simulation theorem.  The native public theorem is stated over
the native kernel games and proves the FOSG/native transport needed to
reuse the realization equality.

\begin{theorem}[Finite native Kuhn realization]
\label{thm:kuhn}
For every checked program $g$, every independent mixed profile
$\mu \in \prod_p \PMF{S_p^{\mathrm{pure}}}$ over pure strategies of
$\mathrm{pureKernelGame}(g)$ admits a behavioral profile $\beta$ of
$\mathrm{pmfBehavioralKernelGame}(g)$ with the same payoff-vector outcome
distribution:
\[
  \Kbeh(\beta)
  \;=\;
  \bigl({\textstyle\bigotimes_p}\,\mu_p\bigr) \bind \Kpure.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Finite FOSG Kuhn deviation simulation]
\label{thm:kuhn-devsim}
For the bounded event-graph FOSG presentation of a checked program, the
game form whose strategies are mixed pure reachable strategies
Nash-simulates the game form whose strategies are legal reachable
behavioral strategies.  Equivalently, for every mixed profile $\mu$ and
its canonical behavioral realization $\beta$, every behavioral
unilateral deviation $\beta'_p$ has a mixed pure unilateral deviation
$\mu'_p$ with the same finite-horizon history law.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
On the bounded FOSG carrier, $\beta$ is constructed pointwise on
reachable information states: at each
$I \in \mathrm{ReachableInfoState}(p)$, $\beta_p(I)$ is the posterior
distribution over actions induced by $\mu_p$ conditional on the
history class realizing $I$.  The FOSG proof uses the
\emph{LegalObservable} theorem for checked event-graph FOSGs.  For the
deviation-simulation theorem, the same construction is applied after
substituting the deviating player's behavioral strategy, producing the
matching mixed pure deviation with the same history law.  The native
theorem transports the mixed profile into the FOSG carrier,
applies the FOSG realization theorem, transports the behavioral witness
back to the native round-view carrier, and uses the native/FOSG pure and
behavioral outcome-law equivalence theorems.
\end{proof}

\Cref{thm:kuhn} is the intended contract between the pure-strategy API
and the PMF-behavioral analysis API.  It does not by itself construct an
equilibrium.  Equilibrium transport uses
\Cref{thm:kuhn-devsim}; existence supplies a source mixed equilibrium
from a finite-game fixed-point theorem (\S\ref{sec:fosg-strategies}).
